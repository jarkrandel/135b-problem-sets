    Problems 2 Practice midterm

    Here’s a practice midterm to give you an idea of what to expect. The midterm will be 4 problems of similar types to the practice ones.
    🔗

    1.

    A casino gives you a fair 4-sided die with faces numbered \(1,2,3,\) and \(4\text{.}\) You repeatedly roll the die \(n\) times, and on each roll you win in dollars the number you rolled. For any \(1 \leq i \leq n\text{,}\) let \(X_i\) be the number of dollars you win on roll \(i\text{.}\)
    🔗
    1. Compute the moment generating function \(\phi_{S_n}(t)\) where \(S_n = X_1 + \cdots +X_n\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. For which values \(a > 0\) can you find an upper bound for \(\prob(S_n\geq an)\) that decays exponentially quickly in \(n\text{?}\) Describe how you would find such a bound, but do not attempt to compute it explicitly.
      🔗
      🔗
    Solution.
    1. Since \(X\) takes the values \(1,2,3,4\) with equal probability, we have
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} \phi_{X_i}(t) = \E(e^{tX}) = \frac{1}{4}(e^t + e^{2t} + e^{3t} + e^{4t}). \end{equation*}
      Then,
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} \phi_{S_n}(t) = \phi_{X_1}(t)^n = \left(\frac{1}{4}(e^t + e^{2t} + e^{3t} + e^{4t})\right)^n. \end{equation*}
      🔗
    2. Here, \(\mu = \E(X_1) = \frac{1+2+3+4}{4} = 2.5\text{,}\) so we can find such a bound for any \(a > 2.5\) by the Large Deviations theorem. This bound has the form
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} \prob(S_n \geq an) \leq e^{-nI(a)} \end{equation*}
      where \(I(a) = \sup\{at - \log(\frac{1}{4}(e^t + e^{2t} + e^{3t} + e^{4t})):t>0\} > 0\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗

    2.

    The same casino from problem 1 gives you a fair 5-sided die with sides numbered \(1,2,3,4,\) and \(5\text{,}\) and they offer you the following game: Roll the die repeatedly until you roll a \(5\text{.}\) When you roll a \(5\text{,}\) you win \(\$15\) and the game ends. When you roll \(1,2,3,\) or \(4\text{,}\) you must pay the casino in dollars the number you rolled and then roll again. Let \(W\) be your random winnings. Compute \(\E(W)\) and \(\var(W)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    We can model our winnings as
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} W = 15 - S = 15 - \sum_{i=1}^N X_i \end{equation*}
    where \(N\) is the number of times we roll \(1,2,3,4\) before rolling a 5, and \(X_i\) is the amount we lose on roll \(i\text{.}\) We can immediately conclude that \(N+1\sim\geom(1/5)\) (we do not want to count the final ``success’’ roll in \(N\) since we don’t lose on that roll). Since \(1,2,3,4\) are all equally likely assuming we rolled one of them, we can conclude that \(X_i\) takes each of them with probability \(1/4\text{.}\) (In general, we could also compute this as a conditional probability
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \prob(X_i = 1) = \prob(\text{ roll 1 } \mid \text{ roll 1,2,3 or 4}) = \frac{\prob(\text{roll 1 and roll 1,2,3, or 4})}{\prob(\text{roll 1,2,3, or 4})} = \frac{\frac{1}{5}}{\frac{4}{5}} = \frac{1}{4}, \end{equation*}
    which works for \(2,3,4\) in place of \(1\) as well.)
    🔗
    Now, we need to compute \(\E(X_i),\var(X_i), \E(N)\) and \(\var(N)\text{.}\) We know that \(\E(X_i) = \frac{1+2+3+4}{4} = 2.5\) and
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \var(X_i) = \E(X_i^2)-\E(X_i)^2 = \frac{1^2+2^2+3^2+4^4}{4} - \frac{25}{4} = \frac{5}{4}. \end{equation*}
    Since \(N+1\sim\geom(1/5)\text{,}\) we immediately get \(\E(N) = 5-1 = 4\) and \(\var(N) = \var(N+1) = \frac{1-(1/5)}{(1/5)^2} = \frac{4\cdot25}{5} = 20\text{.}\) This means
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \var(W) &= \var(15-S) = \var(S) \\ &= \var(X_i)\E(N) + \E(X_i)^2\var(N)\\ &= \frac{5}{4}\cdot4 + \frac{25}{4}\cdot20 = 130. \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    Finally,
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \E(W) = 15-\E(S) = 15-\E(X_i)\E(N) = 15 - \frac{5}{2}\cdot4 = 5. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    3.

    You are an accountant who concentrates for a random number of minutes \(T\sim\expo(\lambda)\) when doing a tax calculation. Given that you concentrate for \(t\) minutes, the error you make in the dollar amount your company owes to the IRS is a random number \(X\sim\uniform(-e^{-t},e^{-t})\) (in millions of dollars).
    🔗
    1. Compute the joint density \(f_{X,T}(x,t)\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Compute \(\E(X)\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    Solution.
    1. Since \(X\) is \(\uniform(-e^{-t},e^{-t})\) given \(T=t\text{,}\) we know \(f_X(x|T=t) = 1/2e^{-t}\) (since \(2e^{-t}\) is just the length of the interval). So,
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} f_{X,T}(x,t) &= f_X(x|T=t)f_T(t)\\ &= \begin{cases} \frac{1}{2e^{-t}}\cdot\lambda e^{-\lambda t} = \frac{\lambda}{2}e^{(1-\lambda)t} & \text{if } -e^{-t}\leq x \leq e^{-t},\ 0\leq t < \infty\\ 0 & \text{otherwise.} \end{cases} \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
      🔗
    2. Since \(T = t\) implies \(X\sim\uniform(-e^{-t},e^{-t})\) we know that \(\E(X|T=t) = (e^{-t}+(-e^{-t}))/2 = 0\) for any \(t > 0\text{.}\) By the law of total expectation,
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} \E(X) = \int_{-\infty}^\infty\E(X|T=t)f_T(t)\,dt = \int_{-\infty}^\infty0\cdot\lambda e^{-\lambda t}\,dt = 0. \end{equation*}
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    4.

    You track the price of a stock over \(n\) days numbered \(1,2,\ldots,n\text{.}\) On each day, the stock has price either \(1,2,\) or \(3\) with equal probability. Let \(P_i\) denote the price on day \(i\text{.}\) For any \(2\leq i \leq n\text{,}\) we say that the stock stayed up on day \(i\) if \(P_i = P_{i-1} = 3\text{.}\) Let \(N_n\) be the number of days the stock stayed up.
    🔗
    1. Compute \(\E(N_n)\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Compute \(\var(N_n)\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    3. Determine with proof a constant \(c\) such that \(\frac{1}{n}N_n\rightarrow c\) in probability.
      🔗
      🔗
    Solution.
    1. For any \(2 \leq i \leq n\text{,}\) define
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} I_i = I_{\{\text{stock stayed up on day } i\}}. \end{equation*}
      Then,
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \E(N_n) &= \sum_{i=2}^n\E(I_i) = (n-1)\cdot\prob(\text{stock stayed up on day 2})\\ &= (n-1)\cdot\prob(P_1=3,P_2=3) = (n-1)\cdot\frac{1}{3}\cdot\frac{1}{3}\\ &= \frac{n-1}{9}. \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
      🔗
    2. Notice that if \(i\) and \(j\) are such that \(|i-j| > 1\text{,}\) then \(I_i\) and \(I_j\) are independent. On the other hand, \(\E(I_iI_{i+1}) = \prob(P_{i-1}=P_i=P_{i+1}=3) = \frac{1}{27}\) so that
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} \cov(I_i,I_{i+1}) = \E(I_{i}I_{i+1}) - \E(I_i)\E(I_{i+1}) = \frac{1}{27}-\frac{1}{81} = \frac{2}{81}. \end{equation*}
      This means
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \var(N_n) &= \sum_{i=2}^n\var(I_i) + 2\sum_{i<j}\cov(I_i,I_j)\\ &= \sum_{i=2}^n\E(I_i^2) - \E(I_i)^2 + \sum_{i=2}^{n-1}\cov(I_i,I_{i+1})\\ &= \sum_{i=2}^n\frac{1}{9}-\frac{1}{81} + \sum_{i=2}^{n-1}\frac{2}{81}\\ &= (n-1)\frac{8}{81} + (n-2)\frac{2}{81}. \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
      🔗
    3. Since \(\E(N_n/n) = \frac{1}{9}\frac{n-1}{n} \xrightarrow{n\rightarrow\infty}\frac{1}{9}\) and
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} \var(N_n/n) = \frac{1}{n^2}\var(N_n) = \frac{n-1}{n^2}\frac{8}{81} + \frac{n-2}{n^2}\frac{2}{81}\xrightarrow{n\rightarrow\infty}0, \end{equation*}
      we can conclude that \(N_n/n\rightarrow \frac{1}{9}\) in probability.
      🔗
      🔗
