    Problem 1: At time step 0, a single red ball and a single blue ball are placed in urns 1 and 2 with some specified initial configuration. At any future time step, we switch a ball from one urn to the other in the following way: We first choose either urn 1 with probability \(p\) or urn 2 with probability \(1-p\text{.}\) Then, we choose a ball randomly in the urn to transfer to the other urn (If there are no balls in the chosen urn, we do nothing.).
    🔗
    1. Compute the transition matrix for this Markov chain. To keep things standardized, use the following convention: state 1 = (RB)(), state 2 = (R)(B), state 3 = (B)(R), state 4 = ()(RB) where (RB)() means the red and blue ball are both in urn 1 and no balls are in urn 2, etc.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Assume that the chain starts with both balls in urn 1. Compute the probability that both balls are in urn 1 after two steps.
      🔗
      🔗
    3. Assume that the chain is equally likely to start in either state with two nonempty urns. Compute the probability that both balls are in urn 2 after 5 steps (you can leave this one as a matrix product).
      🔗
      🔗
    Solution:
    🔗
    1. The transition matrix is
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} P = \begin{bmatrix} 1-p & p/2 & p/2 & 0 \\ 1-p & 0 & 0 & p \\ 1-p & 0 & 0 & p \\ 0 & (1-p)/2 & (1-p)/2 & p \end{bmatrix}. \end{equation*}
      🔗
    2. The matrix \(P^2\) describes the transition probabilities between states after two iterations take place, and the probability we want is just the probability of transitioning from state 1 to itself after two iterations. This means we want to know the matrix element \((P^2)_{11}\text{.}\) This is the same as the dot product of the first row of \(P\) with its first column, which is
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} (P^2)_{11} = (1-p)^2 + 2(1-p)\frac{p}{2} = 1-p. \end{equation*}
      🔗
    3. In this case, our initial distribution row vector is
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} \alpha = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1/2 & 1/2 & 0 \end{bmatrix}. \end{equation*}
      The matrix \(P^5\) contains transition probabilities between states after 5 steps, so the 4-dimensional row vector \(\alpha \cdot P^5 = \begin{bmatrix} v_1 & v_2 & v_3 & v_4 \end{bmatrix}\) has \(v_i =\) the probability that the chain landed in state \(i\) after 5 steps. Our answer is \(v_4\text{,}\) which we can write as
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} (\begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1/2 & 1/2 & 0 \end{bmatrix} \cdot P^5)_4 = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1/2 & 1/2 & 0 \end{bmatrix} \cdot P^5 \cdot \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \\ 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}. \end{equation*}
      🔗
    Problem 2: A particle occupies one of three energy states with energy values \(1,2,\) and \(3\text{.}\) Suppose the particle transitions between states every nanosecond as a Markov chain \((X_n)\) where \(X_n = \)the particle’s energy at nanosecond \(\)n\(\)\(\text{.}\) The particle transitions according to the following transition matrix:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} P = \begin{bmatrix} 1/3 & 1/3 & 1/3 \\ 1/2 & 1/4 & 1/4 \\ 1/6 & 1/2 & 1/3 \end{bmatrix}. \end{equation*}
    Assume that the particle is equally likely to begin in any state at nanosecond 0. In the parts below, "write an expression for X" means write a mathematical expression using the transition matrix and/or \(n\)-step transition probabilities. You do not need to come to a final numerical value for the answer as long as your answer is computable in terms of known quantities.
    🔗
    1. Compute the probability that the particle stays in the same state from nanosecond 0 through nanosecond 9 (inclusive). You do not have to attempt to simplify the expression you get.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Write an expression for the probability that the particle has energy at least 2 at nanosecond 5.
      🔗
      🔗
    3. Write an expression for the particle’s expected energy at nanosecond 5.
      🔗
      🔗
    4. Suppose now that the particle begins with energy 1 at nanosecond 0. Write an expression for the probability that the particle has energy 1 at nanosecond 3 and energy at most 2 at nanosecond 10.
      🔗
      🔗
    Solution:
    🔗
    1. \(X_0 = 1,2\) or \(3\) with equal probability. If \(X_0 = 1\text{,}\) we will have \(\mathrm{P}(X_0 = X_1 = \cdots X_9)\) if and only if \(X_i = 1\) for all \(0 \leq i \leq 9\text{,}\) meaning that the chain transitions from state 1 to itself for the first nine iterations. The probability of this occurring is \(\mathrm{P}(X_0=1)\cdot (P_{11})^9\text{.}\) The same logic works for when \(X_0 = 2\) or \(3\) so that our desired probability is
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} \mathrm{P}(X_0=1)\cdot (P_{11})^9 + \mathrm{P}(X_0=2)\cdot (P_{22})^9 + \mathrm{P}(X_0=3)\cdot (P_{33})^9 = \frac{1}{3}\left(\frac{1}{3^9} + \frac{1}{4^9} + \frac{1}{3^9}\right). \end{equation*}
      🔗
    2. This is just \(\mathrm{P}(X_5 \geq 2) = \mathrm{P}(X_5=2) + \mathrm{P}(X_5 = 3)\text{.}\) The initial distribution has \(\frac{1}{3}\) in each coordinate so that
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix} 1/3 & 1/3 & 1/3 \end{bmatrix}\cdot P^5 = \begin{bmatrix} \mathrm{P}(X_5=1) & \mathrm{P}(X_5 = 2) & \mathrm{P}(X_5=3) \end{bmatrix}. \end{equation*}
      We can extract the sum we need from this row vector by multiplying by a column vector (taking a dot product), so that the expression we need is
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix} 1/3 & 1/3 & 1/3 \end{bmatrix}\cdot P^5\cdot \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}. \end{equation*}
      🔗
    3. We’re now looking for \(\mathrm{E}(X_5) = 1\cdot \mathrm{P}(X_5=1) + 2\cdot \mathrm{P}(X_5=2) + 3\cdot \mathrm{P}(X_5=3)\text{.}\) Reusing our work from the previous part, we see that this is just
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix} 1/3 & 1/3 & 1/3 \end{bmatrix}\cdot P^5\cdot \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \\ 3 \end{bmatrix}. \end{equation*}
      🔗
    4. The question is asking for the probability that the particle transitions from state 1 to state 1 in three steps, then from state 1 to either state 1 or state 2 in 7 additional steps. So, this is
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} P_{11}^3(P_{11}^7 + P_{12}^7). \end{equation*}
      🔗
    Problem 3: You have two coins. Coin 1 has probability 1/2 of Heads, and Coin 2 has probability 3/4 of Heads. You begin by flipping Coin 1 twice, and you flip future coins according to the following rule: You flip Coin 1 unless your previous two flips were Tails, in which case you flip Coin 2 instead. Given that your 6th flip is a Heads, what is the probability that you used Coin 2 on the 6th flip? (You can leave your answer in terms of known matrices and vectors).
    🔗
    Hint: To know whether you used Coin 2, you just need to know what the last two flips were.
    🔗
    Solution: Using the definition of conditional probability, we have
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \mathrm{P}(\text{used Coin 2 for flip $6$}\ |\ \text{flip $6$ was Heads}) = \frac{\mathrm{P}(\text{used Coin 2 for flip 6 and got Heads})}{\mathrm{P}(\text{flip 6 was Heads})}. \end{equation*}
    We will use Coin 2 for flip 6 if and only if flips 5 and 4 were both tails, in which case the probability of flipping Heads is 3/4. Therefore
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \mathrm{P}(\text{used Coin 2 for flip 6 and got Heads}) = \frac{3}{4}\mathrm{P}(\text{flips 5 and 4 were Tails}). \end{equation*}
    We can set up a Markov chain to keep track of the last two flips, and write our probabilities in terms of this chain’s transition matrix and initial distribution. Let state 1 be HH, let state 2 be TH, let state 3 be HT, and let state 4 be TT. We assumed that we used Coin 1 for the first two flips, so we will not model this part with our Markov chain. Let
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} X_i = \text{The state after the $i+2$-th roll} \end{equation*}
    so that, for example, \(X_0 = 2\) if and only if roll 2 was a Tails and roll 1 was a Heads. Because we flip Coin 2 after step \(i\) exactly when \(X_i = 4\text{,}\) the transition matrix is
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} P = \begin{bmatrix} 1/2 & 1/2 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1/2 & 1/2 \\ 1/2 & 1/2 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 3/4 & 1/4 \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
    We can use this to compute our needed probabilities given that we know the initial distribution \(\alpha \) for \(X_0\text{.}\) Since we just flipped Coin 1 twice for the first two flips, all possibilities are equally likely and
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \alpha = \begin{bmatrix} 1/4 & 1/4 & 1/4 & 1/4 \end{bmatrix}. \end{equation*}
    We have
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \mathrm{P}(\text{flips 5 and 4 were Tails}) = \mathrm{P}(X_3 = 4) = (\alpha \cdot P^3)_4 \end{equation*}
    On the other hand, flip 6 was Heads exactly when either \(X_6 = 1\) or \(X_6 = 3\text{,}\) so
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \mathrm{P}(\text{flip 6 was Heads}) =\mathrm{P}(X_4 = 1) + \mathrm{P}(X_4 = 3) = (\alpha \cdot P^4)_1 + (\alpha \cdot P^4)_3. \end{equation*}
    This means our final answer is
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \mathrm{P}(\text{used Coin 2 for flip $6$}\ |\ \text{flip $6$ was Heads}) = \frac{\frac{3}{4}(\alpha \cdot P^3)_4}{(\alpha \cdot P^4)_1 + (\alpha \cdot P^4)_3}. \end{equation*}
